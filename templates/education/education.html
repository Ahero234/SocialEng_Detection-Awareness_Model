Understanding Types of Social Engineering Threats

Learn how attackers manipulate individuals to steal sensitive information and how to defend against these attacks.

Phishing

Phishing is an attack where cybercriminals disguise themselves as trustworthy entities (banks, companies, colleagues) to steal personal details, passwords, or financial data.

How to Identify Phishing:

  • Check sender addresses for inconsistencies.
  • Hover over links before clicking.
  • Beware of urgent or threatening messages.
  • Look for spelling and grammar mistakes.

Spear Phishing

A more targeted version of phishing where attackers research their victims and personalize messages to appear even more legitimate.

How to Spot Spear Phishing:
  • Emails reference personal details that aren't public.
  • Messages appear to come from someone you trust.
  • Requests for immediate action (e.g., "update your account now!").
  • Verify requests for sensitive data by contacting the sender directly.

Pretexting

The attacker creates a plausible but fake scenario to manipulate the victim into revealing sensitive information. This technique relies on building trust and credibility, often by impersonating someone of higher authority such as, IT staff, bank officials, or law enforcement. The attackers use psychological tactics to create a sense of urgency and legitimacy to deceive their victims.

Common Pretexting Techniques:

  • Fake IT support calls asking for login credentials.
  • Scammers posing as HR requesting personal details.
  • Impersonating banks to "verify" account information.
How to Protect Yourself:
  • Verify unknown callers before sharing details.
  • Never disclose passwords or personal information over the phone.
  • Contact the organization directly to confirm legitimacy.

Baiting

Attackers use physical or digital "bait" (free USBs, software, downloads) to trick victims into installing malware or revealing information.

Examples of Baiting:

  • Leaving infected USB drives labeled "Confidential" in public places.
  • Offering free music, movies, or software downloads laced with malware.
  • Fake pop-up ads tricking users into clicking dangerous links.
How to Stay Safe:
  • Never plug in unknown USB devices.
  • Download software only from trusted sources.
  • Avoid "too good to be true" offers online.

Tailgating (Piggybacking)

An unauthorised person closely follows an authorised person into an area containing sensitive information or valuable assets in person. But tailgating can also be a digital tactic, such as when a person leaves a computer unattended while still logged in to a private account or network.

Common Tailgating Techniques:

  • Pretending to have forgotten their ID badge.
  • Carrying items to appear in need of help (e.g., "Can you hold the door for me?").
How to Prevent Tailgating:
  • Ensure doors close behind you in secure areas.
  • Challenge individuals who attempt to enter without verification.
  • Report suspicious activity to security.

ATM Skimming

Attackers attach small devices to ATMs to steal card details and PINs.

How ATM Skimming Works:

  • Fake card readers copy your card information.
  • Hidden cameras record PIN entries.
  • Skimmers may be installed on gas station payment terminals.
How to Stay Safe:
  • Inspect ATMs for loose or unusual attachments.
  • Cover your PIN when entering it.
  • Use ATMs in well-lit, high-traffic areas.

Quid Pro Quo Attacks

Attackers offer something valuable (e.g., free software, fake IT support) in exchange for sensitive information.

Examples of Quid Pro Quo Attacks:

  • Scammers posing as "Microsoft Support" asking for login details.
  • Fake "research surveys" asking for personal data.
How to Protect Yourself:
  • Never provide credentials in exchange for "free services."
  • Verify IT support calls with your organization.
  • Be cautious of unsolicited help or offers.
