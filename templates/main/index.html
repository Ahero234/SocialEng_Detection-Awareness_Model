Stay Safe from Social Engineering Attacks

Learn how cybercriminals trick people and how to protect yourself.

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering attack is a manipulative tactic used by cybercriminals to deceive people into revealing sensitive information, allowing access to systems that are restricted, or performing activities that threaten security. Social engineering targets human psychology, taking advantage of trust, fear, urgency, or curiosity to trick victims, in contrast to typical cyberattacks that take advantage of software or hardware vulnerabilities.

These attacks can take many different forms, such as fraudulent phone calls emails, USB drives that are infected with malware and left in public areas, etc. To gain trust, attackers frequently take on the identities of trusted individuals such as IT support employees, managers, or even law enforcement.

Below are some of most common types of social engineering attacks:

Phishing

Fake emails and messages designed to steal personal information.

Pretexting

Attackers create a false identity to trick victims into giving up sensitive data.

Baiting

Cybercriminals leave malware-infected media to lure victims into installing malicious software.

Why is Social Engineering So Dangerous?

Social Engineering attacks tend to be successful because they go over conventional security protocols. If an employee thinks the request is genuine, they will voluntarily give up their password despite the most advanced firewalls and antivirus programs. Approximately 68% of security breaches involve human errors. This statistic highlights the ongoing vulnerability of individuals/organisations to cybersecurity breaches which often occur due to human errors, such as weak passwords or falling victim to deceptive tactics used by cybercriminals.

How Can You Protect Yourself?

Because social engineering preys on human behavior, awareness and caution are the best defenses. Here'ss how you can stay safe:

  • Do not click on suspicious links or download unknown attachments.
  • Educate yourself and your team on common scams and red flags.
  • Be skeptical of urgent or unusual requests, even from seemingly trusted sources.
  • Use multi-factor authentication (MFA) to protect accounts from unauthorised access.

    • Recognizing Suspicious Emails

    Red Flags in Emails:
  • Urgency or threats (e.g., "Your account will be closed!")
  • Requests for personal or financial information
  • Suspicious sender addresses (e.g., `"yourcompany-secure.com" instead of "yourcompany.com"`)
  • Unknown and insecure links
